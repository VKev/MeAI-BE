# Visual Design Best Practices

> Layout, typography, and composition rules for static images and short-form video. Apply to image-generation prompts, carousel design, and reel framing.
> Last reviewed: 2026-05-03

## Rule of Thirds

**Meaning:** Divide the frame into a 3×3 grid. Place the subject and key elements on the gridlines or their intersections, not centered.

### When to use

- Photo composition for feed posts
- Image-generation prompts (specify "subject placed on the right third")
- Reel framing — the subject's eyes should sit on the upper third gridline

### Why it works

Off-center compositions feel dynamic and create visual tension that holds attention longer than centered compositions. The eye naturally lands on intersection points first.

## F-Pattern Reading

**Meaning:** On text-heavy images, the eye scans in an F shape — top horizontal sweep, second horizontal sweep below, then vertical scan down the left side.

### When to use

- Carousel slides with body text
- Quote graphics
- Infographic posts
- Any image where text is the primary content

### Application

- Put the most important word or number on the **top-left**
- Put the secondary message on the second line
- Avoid hiding the punchline in the bottom-right where eyes never reach

### Why it works

The F-pattern is a documented eye-tracking finding (Nielsen Norman Group studies on web reading behavior) that holds for static images as well. Designs that fight the pattern lose readability.

## Mobile-First (9:16)

**Meaning:** Reels, Stories, TikTok, and Shorts default to vertical full-screen 9:16. Design with that aspect ratio in mind from the start.

### When to use

- All short-form video
- Story content
- IGTV / FB Reels / YouTube Shorts

### Tactics

- Frame subjects in the center vertical band — outside thirds get cropped on some devices
- Reserve top 15% and bottom 25% as "safe zones" — platform UI overlays the rest
- Avoid landscape footage cropped to vertical — always shoot/render native vertical

### Why it works

Vertical video gets ~9x more engagement than horizontal on mobile feeds. Native vertical avoids letterboxing and the "low-effort repurpose" feel.

## Square (1:1) for Feed

**Meaning:** Standard Instagram and Facebook feed posts default to 1:1 square (or 4:5 portrait). Don't repurpose 16:9 horizontal for feed.

### When to use

- Instagram feed posts
- Facebook feed posts
- Carousel slides

### Tactics

- Use 1080×1080 (square) or 1080×1350 (portrait, 4:5) — these get the most feed real estate
- For carousels: pick one aspect ratio and stick with it across all slides

### Why it works

Portrait (4:5) takes up ~25% more vertical screen space than square in mobile feeds, which directly increases dwell time and engagement. Mixed-aspect carousels look unprofessional.

## Text Overlay Rules

**Meaning:** Text on images/video must be readable in 1 second on a small screen.

### When to use

- Reel cover text
- Carousel slide headlines
- Any image with overlaid copy

### Tactics

- **Contrast:** Text color must contrast strongly with background. Use a semi-transparent box behind text if the background is busy.
- **Size:** At least 60pt for reel covers, 80pt for carousel slide 1. Test at 200×356 px (mobile feed thumbnail size).
- **Placement:** Top-left or center — avoid bottom (UI overlays cover it on most platforms).
- **Length:** Max 6–8 words per line. Two lines max for reel covers.
- **Font:** Sans-serif for digital. Avoid thin weights — they disappear at thumbnail size.

### Why it works

Text that requires effort to read is text that doesn't get read. Mobile users decide in <1 second whether to keep watching; unreadable hooks cost reach.

## Brand Color Discipline

**Meaning:** Use 2–3 colors maximum, repeated consistently across every post.

### When to use

- All brand and creator accounts
- Especially important for new accounts building recognition

### Tactics

- Pick: 1 dominant color (60% of frame), 1 accent (30%), 1 highlight (10%)
- Save the palette as a Figma / Canva swatch and reuse on every post
- Avoid trendy color shifts — pick a palette and commit for at least 90 days

### Why it works

Visual consistency makes your posts recognizable mid-scroll without the user reading the handle. After 30+ posts in the same palette, your account becomes pattern-detectable and follow-rate climbs.

## Font Pairing

**Meaning:** Use one display font (for headlines) and one body font (for everything else). No more.

### When to use

- All text-on-image content
- Carousel slides
- Reel text overlays

### Tactics

- **Display:** A bold, geometric sans-serif (e.g., Inter Bold, Poppins Bold, Bebas Neue). Used only for headlines and slide 1.
- **Body:** A clean readable sans-serif (e.g., Inter Regular, Roboto, system default). Used for everything else.
- Never use 3+ fonts in a single post — it looks amateur and fights for attention.

### Why it works

Typography hierarchy guides the eye. Two fonts give enough contrast to differentiate "this is important" vs "this is detail" without becoming visual noise.

## Carousel Pacing

**Meaning:** Each slide must earn the next swipe. Treat slides like cliffhanger episodes.

### When to use

- All multi-slide posts (Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn)

### Structure

- **Slide 1:** Hook — bold visual + numbered promise + "swipe to see"
- **Slides 2 to N–1:** One single idea per slide, big text, minimal decoration. End each slide with a forward-pull ("but here's the catch…", "the trick is on the next slide", or a continuation)
- **Last slide:** Single CTA — "save this", "follow for more", or a clear next action. Don't dilute with multiple CTAs.

### Why it works

Carousel completion rate is a strong distribution signal — the algorithm shows your post to more people if viewers swipe through. Each slide is a micro-test of whether you've earned the swipe.

## Thumbnail Optimization

**Meaning:** Your post is judged at thumbnail size (200px wide on most feeds) before anyone reads it. Design for that size first.

### When to use

- Reel covers
- Carousel slide 1
- Static feed images

### Tactics

- Test the design at 200px wide — if you can't read the headline or recognize the subject, redesign
- Avoid thin lines, small text, busy backgrounds
- Use bold colors that pop in a feed (yellow, red, electric blue)
- Subjects with faces outperform abstract or scenic shots — eyes draw eyes

### Why it works

The thumbnail is the only thing competing for attention in the feed. Most posts are designed at full size and are unreadable at thumbnail size — a post that survives thumbnail scrutiny gets disproportionate clicks.

---

## Negative Space

**Meaning:** Let the subject breathe. Resist the urge to fill every pixel with information.

### When to use

- All visual design
- Especially photo posts and minimal-text quote graphics

### Tactics

- Aim for 30–50% of the frame to be empty (background, sky, single-color fill)
- One subject per frame. Multiple competing subjects split attention and weaken impact.
- White space (or any single-color space) around text makes it feel premium

### Why it works

Cluttered designs look amateur and are harder to read. Negative space increases perceived quality and improves comprehension speed. Apple's product shots and high-end fashion ads are built almost entirely on negative space — it signals confidence in the subject.
