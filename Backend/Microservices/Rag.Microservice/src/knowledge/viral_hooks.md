# Viral Hook Frameworks

> Best-practice heuristics for capturing attention in the first 1–3 seconds of a post or video. Pick a hook that matches the content type and the audience's emotional state.
> Last reviewed: 2026-05-03

## 3-Second Rule

**Meaning:** Deliver a visual + emotional payoff before the viewer's thumb reaches to scroll.

### When to use

- Reels, TikTok, Stories, Shorts — any short-form video
- Any time you need to stop the scroll on autoplay feeds

### Example

Open with a face mid-laugh, a finished plate of food being unveiled, or "Wait — watch this" before a reveal. Avoid 2-second logo intros and slow fades.

### Why it works

Mobile feeds are designed for scrolling. The platform measures the first 3 seconds of view time as the strongest signal of whether to keep distributing the video. Lose the first 3 seconds and the algorithm stops boosting.

## Pattern Interrupt

**Meaning:** Open with something the viewer didn't expect — a contradiction, a controversial take, an abrupt cut, or a visual that breaks feed convention.

### When to use

- Saturated topics where most posts look alike
- Audiences fatigued by formulaic content
- Educational or opinion content

### Example

"Stop posting every day on Instagram." (in a creator-growth feed where everyone says "post every day")
A black frame followed by sudden full-volume audio.
A talking head that suddenly cuts to a completely different scene.

### Why it works

The brain's pattern-matching reflex pauses scrolling when expectations break. Even a half-second pause is enough to register the hook.

---

## Curiosity Gap

**Meaning:** Tease a piece of information without revealing it, forcing the viewer to keep watching to close the loop.

### When to use

- Listicle content ("3 things I wish I knew…")
- Behind-the-scenes / reveal videos
- Tutorial content

### Example

"3 mistakes I made before I crossed 100k followers — #2 changed everything."
"This is the only marketing tactic that actually worked for my 7-figure business."
"I tried [thing] for 30 days. The result wasn't what I expected."

### Why it works

The Zeigarnik effect: the brain assigns higher cognitive priority to incomplete information. Viewers feel mild discomfort until the loop closes — they keep watching to relieve it.

---

## Open Loop

**Meaning:** Pose a specific question at the start, answer it at the end. Variant of Curiosity Gap, but more disciplined.

### When to use

- Educational reels and threads
- Story-based posts
- Long-ish (30s+) videos where retention is the bottleneck

### Example

Open: "Why do some founders reply to every comment but others ignore them?"
Body: explore both sides, share your view.
Close: explicit answer + why.

### Why it works

Forces the viewer to keep watching for the resolution. Also boosts watch-time which is the dominant ranking signal on most short-form platforms.

## Before/After Tease

**Meaning:** Show the transformation upfront in a single frame or split-second cut — viewer wants to know HOW.

### When to use

- Fitness, beauty, finance, productivity, design
- Anything with a visible result
- Carousel post slide 1

### Example

A split-screen image: messy room → spotless room.
Single-shot: "I went from 0 to 50k followers in 90 days. Here's the playbook."
Reel cold-open: 2-second flash of the finished result, then "let's back up to where this started…"

### Why it works

People click on outcomes, not processes. Showing the destination first earns the right to walk them through the journey.

---

## High-Contrast Cold Open

**Meaning:** Open with a visual or audio shock — bright color flash, sudden silence after loud audio, or vice versa.

### When to use

- Reels and TikToks where attention competes with autoplay neighbors
- Brand content needing visual differentiation

### Example

First frame is pure black, then a vibrant product shot with bass-drop audio.
First 0.5 seconds is a face filling the entire frame, expressionless.
Loud overlapping voices that cut to absolute silence + a single line of text.

### Why it works

Mobile autoplay queues a stream of similar-looking content. A frame that visually breaks the surrounding feed pattern earns a longer eye-fixation, and longer fixation correlates with more watch time.

---

## Direct Address

**Meaning:** Call out the specific role / situation of your target viewer in the first sentence so they know it's for them.

### When to use

- Niche content (B2B, specific industries, lifestyle micro-niches)
- Educational posts with a defined ICP
- Cold outreach style content

### Example

"If you're a freelancer charging less than $5,000 per project, stop scrolling."
"For founders who've launched and gotten zero customers — this one's for you."
"To everyone who's tried Zone 2 cardio and quit after a week…"

### Why it works

Self-identification triggers attention more reliably than generic value claims. The viewer feels seen, which reduces cognitive friction to keep watching.

---

## Counterintuitive Claim

**Meaning:** Open with a statement that challenges a widely-held assumption in your niche.

### When to use

- Thought-leadership content
- Threads / LinkedIn-style posts
- Long-form caption posts

### Example

"You don't need a niche to grow on Instagram." (when most advice says you do)
"Cold outreach beats content marketing for early-stage founders."
"Most productivity advice is making you less productive."

### Why it works

Curiosity + mild outrage = high engagement. Even readers who disagree comment to push back, which boosts the post's distribution.

---

## Numbered Promise

**Meaning:** Lead with a specific number + concrete outcome. Specificity outperforms vague claims.

### When to use

- Listicle / educational content
- Carousel slide 1 / Reel cover text
- Threads opening posts
- Blog/email subject lines

### Example

"7 cold-email lines that got me 23 sales calls."
"3 portfolio mistakes that cost me $40k in clients."
"The 5-minute morning routine that cut my anxiety by half."

### Why it works

Numbers signal a finite, scannable promise. The brain processes "7 things" as a contained commitment of attention, not an open-ended ask. Ultra-specific numbers (23, $40k) also feel more credible than rounded ones (20, $50k).

---

## Stakes-First

**Meaning:** Open with what the viewer will lose by NOT watching, rather than what they'll gain by watching. Loss-aversion is roughly 2x more motivating than equivalent gain.

### When to use

- High-cost decisions (financial, career, health)
- Warning / mistake content
- Audiences already aware of the topic

### Example

"This one mistake on your taxes could cost you $10,000 next April."
"If you're not doing this in your first 30 days as a founder, you're already behind."
"The one Instagram setting that's silently killing your reach."

### Why it works

Loss aversion is a robust cognitive bias: the pain of losing something is psychologically larger than the pleasure of gaining the equivalent. Hooks framed around loss earn higher click-through and watch-through than gain-framed hooks.
